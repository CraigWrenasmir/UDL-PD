/// Before you begin — room and setup

Session overview page open and visible on screen
Hybrid setup confirmed — online participants can see and hear
Tables arranged for group work — 4 to 6 per table
All five act pages tested and loading correctly
Your opening student story in mind — a specific student, not a type
Your closing student story ready — the same student, resolved
Preface Shared Language
10:00 – 10:15 15 minutes Presenter-led

/// Open with — why two frameworks

"Before we do anything today, I want to make sure we're speaking the same language. There are two frameworks underpinning this whole session — Structured Teaching and UDL. You may already know one or both of them. Either way, I want to give you my version."

Keep this brisk. This is framing, not teaching. You're establishing shared reference points, not delivering a lecture.

/// Do — Structured Teaching (4–5 min)

→Define Structured Teaching: an approach to autism education that makes the environment and expectations physically and visually predictable, supporting independence and reducing cognitive load.
→Name the four elements: physical structure, schedules, work systems, task organisation. One sentence each.
→Land the core idea: "When the structure does the communicating, students don't have to rely on verbal instruction or social inference."

/// Do — UDL (4–5 min)

→Define UDL: a framework for designing learning experiences that work for the widest range of learners from the outset — not one lesson modified for students who don't fit.
→Use the architecture analogy: ramps and automatic doors work for everyone, not just people who need "special" access.
→Name the three principles: Engagement (the WHY), Representation (the WHAT), Expression (the HOW).
→Land the key shift: "When a student can't access a lesson, UDL says the barrier is in the design — not in the student."

/// Do — the comparison (2 min)

→Walk through the table below. Keep it brisk — one sentence per row.
→Close with the synthesis: "UDL is a design philosophy for human variability." Then stop.
STRUCTURED TEACHING UNIVERSAL DESIGN FOR LEARNING
Origin Autism-specific Mainstream education
Starting Point The individual student's profile The range of learners in a group
Primary Tool The environment and its physical/visual predictability The design of the learning experience itself
View of Flexibility Consistency and predictability is the support Flexibility and choice is the support
Relationship to Routine Routine reduces cognitive load Routine can become a barrier

/// Watch for

People who want to debate the frameworks right now. Acknowledge it: "Hold that thought — we're going to get there, and it'll land better after you've done some work."
People who feel like you're dismissing Structured Teaching. You're not. Name it explicitly if needed: "I use Structured Teaching as a foundation all day. This is not about replacing it."

/// Transition to Act 1

"Now I want to show you a lesson. It's a real lesson — one you'd recognise. And I'd like us to look at it together before we do anything else."
Act 1 The Mirror
10:00 – 10:30 30 minutes Individual → Whole Room

/// Open with — your student story

Tell the story of a specific student — one who was outside the classroom, or disengaged, or unreachable in a lesson that was otherwise going well. Make it concrete. A name, a moment, a detail.

Don't open with UDL. Don't open with theory. Open with a person. The room will settle into whatever you bring first.

"Today I want us to use that student as our compass. Not as a problem to fix — as a design brief."

/// Do — the lesson (2 min)

→Direct everyone to Act 1 — The Mirror on their devices. Give them 2 minutes to read the lesson. Nothing else yet — just read.

/// Do — table activity (15 min)

→Step 1 — "How would you design this?" (5 min) Each table sketches their approach to the lesson for their students. Their actual practice, not an ideal. Don't frame it as critique — frame it as expertise.
→Step 2 — "Who does this serve well?" (2 min) Which student in their class would thrive in what they just described? A specific name, not a type.
→Step 3 — "Who might find this harder?" (3 min) Same design — who is at the edges of what it currently reaches? Again, a specific student.
→Step 4 — Whole-room harvest (5 min) One observation per table. Capture the patterns on the shared display. Let the room hear its own thinking.

/// Key lines to land

"Start from what you'd actually do. Not the ideal lesson — the real one."

This is how you get honest answers. Permission to be real, not aspirational.

"We're not looking for what's wrong. We're starting from what's already working — and asking who it's not yet reaching."

"Hold that student in mind. They're your design brief for the rest of the day."

This is your close for Act 1. The student they named in Step 3 becomes their compass through Acts 2 and 3.

/// Watch for

Tables who jump straight to redesign. Redirect gently: "Let's stay with who it serves first — the redesign comes later."
Defensiveness about their current practice. You're not critiquing it — make that explicit: "This isn't about what's wrong. Your practice is the foundation."
Vague answers in the harvest ("all students benefit"). Push for specificity: "Name one. Who specifically would thrive here?"
Online participants falling behind. Check in at the harvest — invite one online table to share first.

/// Transition to Act 2

"Before we redesign anything, we're going to understand it properly. I want us to look at this lesson through the eyes of four specific students — not in general, but for real people with real needs."
Act 2 The Analysis
10:30 – 11:10 40 minutes Table Teams

/// Do — set up the activity

→Direct everyone to Act 2 — The Analysis. Assign one student persona per table (or let them choose). They will work on that student for all three rounds.
→Three rounds of 10 minutes each. Each round moves from specific → causal → structural. Call time between rounds.
→Circulate constantly. Listen more than you speak. Your job here is to hear what's emerging, not to lead it.

/// Circulating prompts — use when tables get stuck

"Where exactly does your student get stuck — what is the moment it breaks?"

Round 1 prompt. Push for specificity — not "this lesson is hard for them" but the precise point of failure.

"What does the lesson assume your student can do — that they may not be able to?"

Round 2 prompt. Help tables use the template: "This lesson assumes ___ can ___. But for ___, that breaks because ___."

"Is this a problem for your student — or is it a flaw in how the lesson was designed?"

Round 3 prompt. This is the shift from individual to structural. Push them to name the design flaw, not the student's gap.

"Think about Priya specifically. This lesson is built around visual supports. What does that mean for her?"

Priya's vision impairment is the sharpest tension in the room. If tables haven't landed on it, point them there.

/// Do — share-back

→After Round 3, give tables 1 minute to agree on their key finding — the structural assumption they found.
→Go around the room — 60 seconds per table. Keep to time. Harvest key themes onto the Padlet (QR on screen) as they share.
→Look for: the Priya insight (vision + visual lesson), the Lachlan insight (relevance/purpose), the Malcolm insight (physical access). If they don't surface, name them yourself briefly.

/// Watch for

Tables moving into solutions in Round 1 or 2. Bring them back: "Stay in the diagnosis — what is broken, not how to fix it."
Tables focusing only on Malcolm (most obvious support needs). Gently push: "What about Priya? What about Lachlan?"
Share-back running long. Keep it moving — 60 seconds is enough. You're harvesting themes, not full reports.

/// Transition to Break

"You've just built a shared picture of what this lesson doesn't offer. That's the diagnostic work. After the break, you redesign it."
Break 11:10 – 11:30 · 20 minutes · Use this time to review what you harvested from Act 2 and identify the 2–3 themes you'll weave into Act 4.
Act 3 The Rebuild
11:30 – 12:20 50 minutes Hackathon

/// Open Act 3 with energy

"Right. You've diagnosed the problem. Now you're going to rebuild the lesson — three rounds, 15 minutes each. Each round has a constraint. The constraint is not optional."

Say this with energy. The pace shifts here — Act 3 is the fastest part of the day.

/// Do — run the three rounds

→Direct everyone to Act 3 — The Rebuild. Read the Round 1 constraint aloud: "Every student must have a way in."
→Round 1 — 15 minutes. Circulate. Ask: "Who does this entry point reach? Who doesn't it reach yet?"
→Call time. 2-minute steal. Be explicit: "Look at the table next to you. Take one idea. Go."
→Read Round 2 constraint aloud: "The content must reach every student." Round 2 — 15 minutes.
→Call time. 2-minute steal. Same instruction.
→Read Round 3 constraint aloud: "Every student must be able to show you what they know." Round 3 — 15 minutes.
→Timing discipline is everything in this act. Keep to the clock — the urgency is part of the design.

/// Circulating prompts

"You've kept the structure — good. Now: does Malcolm have a way in? Does Priya?"

"If Lachlan asks 'why are we doing this?' — what's the answer in your redesign?"

"What does 'something real' look like for Sally specifically?"

/// Watch for

Tables ignoring the constraints. Redirect immediately — the constraints are the pedagogical point of this act.
The steal being skipped. Physically prompt it: walk over, point to the neighbouring table's work, say "What's one thing you can take?"
Tables finishing early. Ask: "Which of the four students still isn't reached? Go back for them."

/// Transition to Act 4

"Hold onto what you built. Don't tidy it up — the roughness is where the thinking lives. We're going to hold it up to something now."
Act 4 The Confrontation
12:20 – 12:45 25 minutes Craig-led

/// Do — present the tension (5+2+5+8+5 min)

→5 min: Direct everyone to Act 4 — The Confrontation. Slow the pace deliberately. This is the most Craig-led part of the day. Present the two-card tension on screen.
→2 min: Land the key contrast: "Structured teaching honours what you know. UDL honours what you don't." Say it slowly. Pause. Let it sit.
→5 min: Table discussion — "How should we incorporate student voice into the co-design of our lessons?" Let them discuss. You listen. Harvest one or two responses aloud.
→8 min: Table audit — direct tables to the audit questions with their Act 3 redesign in front of them.
→5 min: Whole-room share — one finding per table. Keep it moving. You're after insight, not reporting.

/// Key lines — land these carefully

"Structured teaching honours what you know. UDL honours what you don't. These are not opposites — structured teaching is already doing most of this work. UDL asks you to flex it at the edges."

"We don't remove structure. We design it so more students can enter it."

This is the single most important line of the day. Pause after it.

"Look at what you built in Act 3. Where does your own preference show up in it? That's not a criticism — that's the work."

"Some of our most structured classrooms are already halfway to UDL. They just haven't been given permission to flex."

Use this as your close for Act 4 — it's affirming and forward-looking.

/// Watch for

Anyone feeling that structured teaching is being criticised. Explicitly name what it gets right — it's on the page — before introducing the tension.
The audit running long. Keep table discussion to 8 minutes — the whole-room share should be brief. The audit is the real work, not the share-back.
The pace being too fast. This act should feel different to Act 3. Slow down deliberately. Silence is fine here.

/// Transition to Act 5

"The last thing I want to do today is leave you with a concept. I want to leave you with a question — one you'll carry into every lesson plan from here."
Act 5 Commit + Close
1:00 – 1:30 30 minutes incl. buffer Individual → Room

/// Do — three rounds (3+5 / 3+5 / 8 min) + share (4 min)

→Direct everyone to Act 5 — Commit + Close. Introduce the structure: three rounds, individual then table.
→Round 1 — Reflect (8 min): 3 min individual quiet, then 5 min table. Prompt: one thing today made you question about how you plan or support learning.
→Round 2 — The Question (8 min): 3 min individual, then 5 min table. Distil the reflection into one question they'll ask before every lesson plan. Tables help sharpen it.
→Round 3 — Apply It (8 min): Individual. Apply their question to a real lesson they're planning next week. What changes?
→Whole-room share (4 min): One question per table — the one they'll actually use. Then close.
→Close with your student story — the same student from the opening, but now with a resolution. What did designing for them make possible?

/// Closing lines

"You came in with a lesson. You leave with a question. That question is the work continuing."

"Design is never finished. It just gets closer to the range."

That's the last thing you say. Then let it sit.

/// Watch for

Questions that are too abstract. Gently push: "What would you actually ask yourself — something specific enough to help you make a decision in the moment?"
Round 2 table conversations going in circles. Prompt: "Which version of your question makes you uncomfortable? That's probably the right one."
Running over time. If you're tight, shorten the whole-room share but protect the closing. The story matters more than the share.
