Act 1 — The Mirror

Living and Non-Living Things

This is today's shared lesson — a starting point, not a finished design. Read through it, then your table will work through the questions below together. Start from your own practice. The thinking emerges from there.

Lesson Intention Science — Biological Sciences

Learning Outcome

Students will distinguish between living and non-living things — identifying what makes something alive, sorting examples, and explaining their reasoning in whatever way best demonstrates their understanding.

Year Level

This outcome applies to students across all years — the depth of thinking scales with the learner, not the lesson design.

How it typically looks

Visuals on the table — symbols, photographs, or line art cut out as sorting cards. Students physically sort them into living and non-living categories using a template or labelled spaces. Clear, structured, predictable — and genuinely good autism teaching.

/// UDL Thinking

UDL Principle 1

Engagement — How are students invited into this learning?

Engagement

What this lesson might already include

Visual sorting cards Symbols, photos or line art Clear task steps Teacher modelling Predictable routine Defined start and finish

Questions to sit with

Is there only one way to enter this learning — or are there several different doors in?

Which of your students wouldn't reach this at all, as it's currently designed?

Does every student need to start the same way, at the same time, with the same stimulus?

UDL Principle 2

Representation — How many ways can students access the content?

Representation

What this lesson might already include

Photograph sorting cards Physical manipulation Two clear categories Teacher-made materials Visual labels

Questions to sit with

If a student can't read the label on a sorting card, can they still access the concept?

Is the visual support the only way in — or is it one of several?

What if a student needs to move, touch, or hear something to understand it?

UDL Principle 3

Expression — How can students show what they know?

Expression

What this lesson might already include

Completed sorting template Teacher observation Written labels or sentences Tick or cross marking

Questions to sit with

Does writing this down prove they understand it — or does it prove they can write?

What does "knowing" look like for your most complex student?

Are you assessing the learning — or the format of the response?

Could a student who uses one switch still show you genuine understanding?
